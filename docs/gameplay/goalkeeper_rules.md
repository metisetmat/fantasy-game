# Goalkeeper Rules

Each team has exactly one goalkeeper.

V0.1 rule:
- the goalkeeper may use hands only inside their own defensive goal area;
- outside that area, the goalkeeper behaves as a normal abstract field participant;
- goalkeeping is still abstract and deterministic, not a physics system.

The goalkeeper participates in:
- goal attempts;
- saves below the crossbar;
- aerial controls;
- rebounds;
- clearances;
- last-line organization;
- long distribution.

Goalkeeper fatigue specialization:
- goalkeeper physical fatigue is separate from outfield fatigue and usually rises slower;
- goalkeeper mental fatigue rises through long inactivity, repeated opponent danger phases, shots faced, rebounds, scrambles, close-score pressure, late-match pressure, defensive disorganization, and previous handling errors;
- goalkeeper readiness states are SET, ALERT, COLD, UNDER_PRESSURE, and OVERLOADED;
- mental fatigue affects concentration, positioning, reaction reliability, decision quality, legal hand-use timing, catch/parry choice, rebound direction, spill probability, second-save recovery, and communication with the defensive line;
- a fresh but cold goalkeeper can react poorly after long inactivity;
- a busy goalkeeper can stay alert, but repeated danger can push the role into OVERLOADED behavior;
- strong goalkeeper attributes mitigate mental fatigue but do not erase it.

Goal frame interaction:
- the goal frame sits on the goal line between the field pressure zone and the in-goal grounding zone;
- below the crossbar between the posts is the goal/net area;
- above the crossbar between the posts is the target for drops and penalties.

Scoring-space rule:
- a try is grounded in the legal in-goal grounding zone, not inside the goal frame;
- a goal is scored through the frame below the crossbar;
- a drop or penalty is scored above the crossbar between the posts;
- a team cannot score a try behind the goal area or behind the goal frame.
